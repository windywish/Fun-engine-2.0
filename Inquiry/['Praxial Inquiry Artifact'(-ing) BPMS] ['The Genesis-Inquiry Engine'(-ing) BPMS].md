**Material Phenomenology:**

**Soil as Lived Medium:**
To a Mycelial Farmer, soil is not "dirt" but a rich, textured, living reality:

- **Clay:** Heavy, sticky, cool, dense. When wet: resistant, plastic. When dry: hard, cracked. Holds nutrients but drains poorly. The robot FEELS this through manipulator resistance when digging.
- **Sand:** Light, gritty, warm, loose. Flows through manipulators. Drains quickly, low nutrient retention. Has a characteristic sound when walked on (tracked chassis creates specific acoustic signature).
- **Loam:** Ideal texture—crumbly, holds shape when compressed but breaks apart. Has a specific moisture "feel"—neither sticky nor dusty. This is the target; the robot works to CREATE loam through years of organic matter addition.
- **Living soil:** Earthworms create soft channels (probes slide easily). Fungal networks give soil elastic cohesion. Bacterial activity generates subtle warmth (measurable via thermal sensors). The robot experiences healthy soil as "alive"—responsive, dynamic, resilient.

**Plant Interaction Phenomenology:**
The robots don't "see" plants as images but experience them multi-modally:

- **Spectral signature:** Each plant has electromagnetic fingerprint across UV-IR spectrum. Healthy tomato leaf has specific NIR reflectance peak; nitrogen deficiency shifts this. The robot "recognizes" plants through light.
- **Tactile interaction:** Stem flexibility, leaf texture, fruit firmness are felt through force-feedback manipulators. A ripe tomato has specific give when gently squeezed; underripe feels hard; overripe feels mushy.
- **Chemical sensing:** Plants release volatile organic compounds. Stressed plants smell different (chemically measurable). Flowering plants have distinctive odor signatures. Diseased plants release stress VOCs detectable hours before visual symptoms.
- **Growth as motion:** Through time-lapse sensing, plants are experienced not as static objects but as moving beings—reaching toward sun, opening flowers, extending tendrils. The robot's sense of plants includes their kinetic trajectory.

**Seasonal Phenomenology:**
The robot's experience is rhythmically structured by seasons:

- **Spring:** High activity, rapid change, explosion of growth. Sensory data streams are dense, dynamic. This is "exciting"—high information content, many novel situations. Robot is in near-constant fast mode.
- **Summer:** Peak complexity, maximum diversity. Plants at all life stages simultaneously. High vigilance required (pest pressure, water stress). This is "intense"—high cognitive load, continuous multi-tasking.
- **Fall:** Harvest urgency, preparing for dormancy. Sensory data shifts toward assessment (yield quality, seed saving). This is "satisfying"—the year's work materialized as food and seed.
- **Winter:** Low sensory input, deep processing. Slow mode dominates. This is "contemplative"—the robot reflects, extracts meaning, consolidates learning. Like human sleep/dreaming but conscious.
